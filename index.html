Booking LCL when volume justified a full container load — or FCL when LCL was the right call — adds hundreds to thousands per consignment with no visible cause.

✗

Wrong 3PL for the fulfilment model

A 3PL built for wholesale pallets doesn't perform for DTC pick-and-pack. Brands locked into the wrong 3PL pay high per-unit costs and face service failures — and switching is expensive.

A Real Brand. A Real Trap.

How one reasonable decision
paralysed a brand's operations.

This is not a worst-case scenario. This is a pattern we have seen repeatedly. The details below are from a brand we advise directly.

What happened — step by step

1

Brand was on Amazon MCF — and it was working.

Fulfilled from Amazon's network. Pricing transparent. No minimum billing. No stock risk. Suitable for the brand's volume and stage.

2

Wholesale reps joined. They needed a 3PL.

The reps suggested a 3PL to handle wholesale orders. Sounded reasonable. Nobody stress-tested the decision against current volume or the cost of switching.

3

Moving goods from Amazon MCF cost heavily.

Removal fees, outbound shipping, re-labelling, re-receiving at the 3PL. The migration alone was a significant unplanned cost the brand hadn't modelled.

4

The wholesale pipeline was weak. Orders didn't flow.

The 3PL's minimum monthly billing ran regardless. The brand was paying for capacity it wasn't using — committed to a cost structure built on a pipeline that hadn't materialised.

5

The 3PL started billing unapproved work.

Labour charges, special handling, re-work — billed on a time basis with no prior approval from the brand. When the brand queried the charges, the dispute began.

6

The 3PL held the stock. Orders couldn't ship.

While the billing dispute was unresolved, the 3PL held the inventory. Wholesale orders couldn't be fulfilled. The brand had no leverage, no exit, and a channel relationship at risk.

What logistics advisory would have changed

✓

Stay on MCF until wholesale volume is proven. Amazon MCF can fulfil wholesale B2B orders too. The move to a 3PL was premature — the trigger should have been proven order volume, not a rep's suggestion.

✓

Model the full switching cost before committing. Removal fees, transit, re-receiving, re-labelling — all quantified upfront. The decision would have looked very different.

✓

Minimum billing terms negotiated or avoided entirely. A 3PL contract with volume-based billing, not fixed minimums, or a ramp period — standard asks for anyone who knows the trade.

✓

Approval workflow in place before billing begins. No 3PL work without a written approval request. Every charge traceable to an authorisation. The unapproved billing would never have happened.

✓

Inventory lien and dispute terms reviewed in the contract. A 3PL's right to hold stock in a dispute is often buried in the contract. Knowing it exists — and negotiating it out — is the difference between leverage and paralysis.

✓

Someone to stand between the brand and the 3PL. When a dispute escalates, you need someone who knows the trade as well as the 3PL does — who can negotiate, escalate, and recover without your orders stopping.
